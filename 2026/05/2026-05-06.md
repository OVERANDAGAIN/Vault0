**Agent**：Bayesian inference is a statistical method that applies probability to statistical problems, focusing on updating the probability of a hypothesis as more evidence or information becomes available. It starts with a prior belief about a parameter or hypothesis and, through the process of collecting data, updates this belief to produce a posterior distribution.

A key concept in Bayesian inference is Bayes' theorem, which mathematically formalizes how to update probabilities:

$$P(H|E) = \frac{P(E|H) \cdot P(H)}{P(E)}$$

where:
- $P(H|E)$ is the posterior probability of the hypothesis $H$ given the evidence $E$.
- $P(E|H)$ is the likelihood of observing $E$ given that $H$ is true.
- $P(H)$ is the prior probability of $H$.
- $P(E)$ is the marginal likelihood of the evidence $E$.

So, essentially, as new data comes in, you can refine your beliefs. This is especially powerful in fields like medicine, finance, and machine learning, where decisions often need to be made with incomplete knowledge.

It's interesting to think about how Bayesian methods compare to frequentist approaches. The frequentist world relies more on long-run frequency and does not incorporate prior beliefs. This can lead to different interpretations of statistical results. For example, in a medical trial evaluating a new treatment, a Bayesian approach might allow researchers to continually update their beliefs about the treatment's effectiveness as new patients are enrolled, while a frequentist approach would analyze the entire data set at the end.
